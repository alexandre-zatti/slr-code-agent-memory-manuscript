A Systematic Literature Review}

\author{
  Alexandre Zatti\thanks{Programa de Pós-Graduação em Computação
  Aplicada (PPGPCA), Universidade Federal da Fronteira Sul (UFFS).}
}

\date{}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
\textbf{Context.}
Coding agents based on Large Language Models (LLMs) suffer from
cross-session knowledge volatility: with every new task, the agent
loses the context accumulated in previous resolutions.
Several persistence architectures have been proposed to mitigate this
limitation, but the field lacks systematic comparison across
alternatives and cross-study cost analysis.
\textbf{Objective.}
To map the literature on knowledge persistence in AI-based coding
agents and to answer five secondary questions covering taxonomy,
evaluation, performance, cost, and architectural complexity.
\textbf{Method.}
Systematic review conducted following PRISMA 2020.
Searches in Scopus and arXiv, supplemented by citation chasing via
Semantic Scholar and one documented manual addition,
identified 1{,}194 records published between 2023 and March 2026.
After duplicate removal and title/abstract/full-text screening, 28
studies were included for structured extraction of 37 fields:
24 propose persistence architectures and 4 are contextual sources
(one analytical survey, two benchmark proposals, one security
evaluation).
One of the 24 architecture studies, Live-SWE-Agent, was retained as a
boundary case: it externalises task-local procedural knowledge as
executable tools but does not persist those tools across tasks.
\textbf{Results.}
Among the 24 architecture studies, experience banks account for 46\%,
the LLM itself acts as read/write controller in 54\%, and hybrid
temporal scope is present in 58\%.
Of the 19 architecture studies with controlled no-persistence
baselines, 18 report positive aggregate results in their main
controlled comparison, while CTIM-Rover reports negative results
across its CTIM configurations;
within the SWE-bench Verified subset ($n=12$, including CTIM-Rover),
the median gain is 6.5 percentage points, with reported gains ranging
from $-$2.0 to +39.0 percentage points.
Eight of the 24 architecture studies (33\%) report no cost data, rising
to 12 of the 28 included studies (43\%) when contextual sources are
counted;
nine studies (32\%) report adverse or cautionary findings related to
persistence---retrieval noise, model-dependent overhead, security, or
evaluation artefacts---and no study compares three or more
architectures on the same benchmark with the same model.
\textbf{Conclusion.}
The absence of head-to-head comparisons and the underreporting of
costs limit both architectural choice and practical assessment.
These findings motivate a controlled comparative study with a shared
benchmark, a fixed model, and standardised cost accounting.
\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords:} systematic review; coding agents; knowledge
persistence; agent memory; cost-efficiency.
