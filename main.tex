  Aplicada (PPGPCA), Universidade Federal da Fronteira Sul (UFFS).
  Corresponding author.}
}

\date{}

\begin{abstract}
\textbf{Context.}
Coding agents based on Large Language Models (LLMs) suffer from
cross-session knowledge volatility: with every new task, the agent
loses the context accumulated in previous resolutions.
Several persistence architectures have been proposed to mitigate this
limitation, but the field remains fragmented, without systematic
comparison across alternatives or cross-study cost analysis.
\textbf{Objective.}
Map the literature on knowledge persistence in AI-based coding agents
and answer five secondary questions covering taxonomy, evaluation,
performance, cost, and architectural complexity.
\textbf{Method.}
Systematic review conducted following PRISMA 2020.
Searches in Scopus and arXiv, supplemented by citation chasing via
Semantic Scholar, identified 1,194 records published between 2023
and March 2026.
After duplicate removal and title/abstract/full-text screening, 28
studies were included for structured extraction of 37 fields.
\textbf{Results.}
Experience banks account for 46\% of primary studies, with the LLM
itself acting as read/write controller in 54\% of the cases and
hybrid temporal scope in 58\%.
The 19 studies with controlled comparison report universal gains over
the no-persistence baseline, with a median of 6.8 percentage points on
SWE-bench Verified and an amplitude of +2.2 to +39.0 percentage points.
Despite these gains, 43\% of the studies omit cost data, 32\% document
memory-induced degradation scenarios, and none compares three or more
architectures on the same benchmark with the same model.
\textbf{Conclusion.}
The absence of direct comparisons and the underreporting of costs limit
both architectural choice and practical assessment.
These findings point to the need for a controlled empirical comparative
study as the field's natural next step.
\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords:} systematic review; coding agents; knowledge
persistence; agent memory; cost-efficiency.
